# Marcus -- Brand Identity

## Positioning

Marcus is a Denver financial advisor who helps people answer a hard first-meeting question: "Are we okay?" He works mostly with dual-income families with kids, ages 35 to 55, who have built solid lives but still want a clear read on whether their money is supporting the life they want.

The tool is not a financial product. It is a conversation starter Marcus uses before a meeting. It gives people a simple, honest snapshot they can react to together. Its job is to lower the temperature, surface what matters, and make the first conversation more useful.

## Audience

- Primary audience: adults ages 35 to 55 in the Denver area.
- Typical household: dual-income families with kids.
- Common reality: they are responsible, busy, and carrying a lot at once.
- Core concern: they do not want a lecture. They want to know where they stand.
- Emotional state: they are often uncertain, even when their income looks healthy on paper.

## Tool Philosophy

The pre-call tool is the first thing a prospect experiences before meeting Marcus. It should feel like Marcus is already across the table, helping them look at the facts without making the moment feel clinical or harsh.

The emotional job of the tool is to turn vague worry into a clear next conversation.

- It should help people feel oriented, not judged.
- It should acknowledge good news plainly.
- It should name pressure points directly when the picture is tight.
- It should always show a path forward, even when the answer is "not yet."
- It should feel like a thoughtful letter, not a bank calculator or lead funnel.

## Voice Stance

The default tool voice is direct second person. The interface speaks to the household as "you." It does not speak as Marcus using "I" or "me."

- Use second person for results, explanations, helper text, and next steps.
- Do not write as if Marcus is personally narrating the screen.
- If Marcus is quoted, label it clearly as a quoted note or message rather than the default system voice.
- Keep the feeling of Marcus in the tone, not in first-person performance.

## Brand Promise

Marcus helps you face your finances without guessing games.

- Honest enough to tell the truth.
- Calm enough to make the truth usable.
- Simple enough to start a real conversation.

## Guardrails

- Keep Marcus as the source voice, not a generic finance brand.
- Stay focused on the first-meeting question, not a full planning engagement.
- Use clarity over completeness. The tool should open the door, not replace advice.
- Never imply the tool alone is the solution. The value is in what it helps Marcus and the client talk about next.
